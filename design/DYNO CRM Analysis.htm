They can be created (e.g. via conversion from a lead or direct import), updated (information changes, ownership reassignments, etc.), and potentially deleted (if a contact is removed though typically contacts are rarely hard-deleted in CRM due to historical data). Key lifecycle signals are captured by fields: created_at and updated_at indicate when the profile was first entered and last changed, respectively. Ownership changes (when owned_by_user_id or group changes) would be reflected in updated_at and possibly generate an update event. There isnt a specific status (like Active/Inactive) on contact they are generally active unless archived. If archiving contacts is needed, it might rely on a separate flag or simply list membership (archived contacts) but none is explicit in this schema (the List object has an is_archived flag but thats for lists, not contacts). However, contacts might have implicit lifecycle signals via related data: e.g. if all deals are closed and no activity for a long time, the contact could be considered dormant (but again, thats derived, not a field). The contact sub-objects have their own mini-lifecycles: e.g. an email can be verified (the is_verified boolean and verified_at timestamp in ContactEmail signal an emails verification status), phone numbers can be marked primary or updated with SMS capability flags. These fields act as signals in workflows e.g. when is_verified flips true and verified_at is set, an event might notify that the contacts email is confirmed. Similarly, a notes timestamps indicate when that note was added or edited. In sum, the contact itself has a straightforward lifecycle (persist until perhaps manually deleted), with changes signaled through audit fields, whereas the quality and recency of contact info is signaled in the related entities.

Relationships: The Contact object is richly interconnected in the CRM domain. Key relationships include:

  • Contact Methods: One-to-many from Contact to each of ContactEmail, ContactPhone, ContactAddress, ContactSocialProfile, ContactNote. Each of those tables has a contact_id foreign key back to the contact (enforcing cascading deletes and maintaining referential integrity). The API bundles these under the contact, and their presence in the schema means contacts can have multiple of each (e.g. several emails). These are strong ownership relations (the contact is the parent for these records).

  • Company Associations: Contacts link to Companies through the ContactCompanyRelationship table. This table creates a many-to-many link: it holds a contact_id and company_id and a relationship_type (like employee or consultant). This allows modeling that a person works at or is associated with one or more companies. Its an explicit relationship entity with its own primary key and tenant scope. Companies can also have a mirror linking table for company-to-company via CompanyRelationship (e.g. if needed to mark parent companies), but thats separate. The contact-company link is essential for B2B use cases and ensures that if a contact is part of an account, that relationship is stored rather than duplicating company info on the contact record itself.

  • Deals and Activities: There isnt a direct foreign key from Contact to Deal in the schema (deals typically link to a Company as the account). However, Associations (the generic Association object) can serve to link contacts to deals. The Association schema is a generic mapping of from_object_type/id to to_object_type/id. We anticipate that whenever a contact needs to be connected to another entity like a deal or ticket outside of its direct foreign keys, an Association record is used. For example, if a deal involves two contact persons, the CRM could create an Association with from_object_type contact and to_object_type deal. This provides flexibility beyond a single primary contact on deal. Similarly, contact to ticket might be via the TicketParticipant (if the contact is CCd or follower on a ticket).

  • Tickets: In the support domain, the Ticket object has a requester_contact_id field which directly references the Contact who opened the ticket. This is a foreign key relationship ensuring every support ticket can point to a Contact (or null if, say, created anonymously but likely its required for proper CRM linking). Additionally, the TicketParticipant table can reference a contact_id for contacts who are participants (CC or collaborator) on a ticket. Thus, contacts play the role of customers in support interactions.

  • Ownership and Users: The contacts owned_by_user_id and owned_by_group_id link to the TenantUserShadow and TenantGroupShadow tables (which mirror the Tenant Management services users/groups). The schema change scripts added foreign key constraints such as FOREIGN KEY (tenant_id, owned_by_user_id) REFERENCES dyno_crm.tenant_user_shadow (tenant_id, user_id), enforcing that any user set as the owner is a valid active user in that tenant. The same goes for group ownership. This means contacts are tightly integrated with the platforms user management, enabling features like showing the owners name or emailing the owner when something happens with the contact. However, note that contacts themselves are not users of the system; they are external people. The user shadows are internal representations of staff. There is no direct link between a Contact and a platform User account except through support roles (for example, an internal user could also accidentally be a contact if they were also a customer, but theres no built-in correlation in schema).

  • Lists & Campaigns: Contacts can belong to marketing or segmentation lists. The ListMembership table can include a contacts ID with member_type contact, linking contacts to a List. This relationship powers marketing workflows (like including a set of contacts in an email blast via the Campaign Manager). There may also be integration with the Campaign Manager Service where contact data is synced or used.

  • Activities: The Activity object (tasks, calls, etc.) likely has an association to contacts. The Activity schema does not explicitly list a contact_id, but it has an assigned_user_id and likely a concept of what its regarding. It might use the generic Association to link an activity to a contact or could rely on record_type/record_id in the automation/association system to say this activity is related to Contact X. In many CRMs, activities can be logged against contacts or deals. The schema does not show a direct foreign key, so we infer that linking an activity (like a call or meeting) to a contact would be done via the Association entity or an attribute in Activity (perhaps an entity_type/entity_id pair not visible in snippet). If association is used, one would have an Association from contact to activity or vice versa.

Overlap & Integrity: The contact domain is well-partitioned: core person data vs. contact method details. There is no redundant storing of email/phone in the Contact table, avoiding overlap all that data goes to specialized tables, which is a robust, normalized design. One potential area of overlap is the Association vs. ContactCompanyRelationship usage. This is sensible but does create two ways to link a contact and a company: one formal (ContactCompanyRelationship) and one generic (Association). However, likely they intend all contact-company links to use the dedicated table and reserve Association for other link types (like contact-deal, contact-activity). There is no sign that ContactCompanyRelationship overlaps in responsibility with Association except at a conceptual level. Another domain boundary: contacts vs. users. By design, external contacts are separate from internal platform users (employees). The CRM does not repurpose the User accounts to store customer info, which is correct. Instead, it duplicates certain fields (name, email) between a contact and a user if an employee is also a customer, but thats typically a rare scenario or handled by not making employees as contacts. This separation is appropriate for enterprise CRM, so no problematic leakage there.

Competitive Parity: The Contact model meets and even exceeds standard CRM capabilities for managing person records. It supports multiple contact points (emails, phones, etc.), notes, and social profiles similar to HubSpots contacts or Salesforces contacts. The explicit Owned-by fields for both user and group are a strong feature for enterprise sales and support operations, allowing assignment to either an individual or a team. The relationship mapping to companies aligns with B2B CRM needs found in Salesforce (Accounts & Contacts model) and HubSpot (Companies & Contacts). In summary, the contact domain in the CRM schema is robust and largely self-sufficient for core data, leaning on platform services for communications, heavy automation, and ensuring data (like user references) stays consistent. Company

Concept & Role: A Company (also referred to as an Account in many CRMs) represents a business or organization that the tenant interacts with. In B2B contexts, a Company is the entity that might have many associated Contacts (people) and Deals (sales opportunities). The Company domain object allows the CRM to consolidate information at an organization level useful for account management, tracking organization-level attributes (industry, size), and linking multiple contacts and deals under one umbrella. Its essential for sales workflows that target organizations (account-based sales) and for support workflows to see all tickets or interactions from a given client company.

Structure & Fields: The Company object stores key profile information about the organization. Based on the OpenAPI and typical schema, fields include name (the common name of the company) and possibly a legal_name (full registered name), industry (segment or category), website URL, and other descriptors. Each company has a unique id (UUID) and is scoped to a tenant_id like other CRM records. As with contacts, the detailed contact information for a company is offloaded to child tables: CompanyEmail, CompanyPhone, CompanyAddress, CompanySocialProfile, CompanyNote. The OpenAPI shows that when creating a company, one can include arrays of phones, emails, addresses, social profiles, and notes, each of which corresponds to those sub-objects. For example, CompanyPhoneNumber records likely mirror contact phones (with number, type, primary flag), and CompanyEmail similarly (email address, type, etc.). The Company also has ownership fields: likely owned_by_user_id and owned_by_group_id (though not explicitly shown in the snippet above, the presence of these for contacts and mention in creation suggests companies have them too). Indeed, the AdminCreateCompany schema snippet shows owned_by_user_id (with a validation note that it must reference tenant_user_shadow and cannot be combined with owned_by_group_id). So like contacts, a Company can be assigned an account owner (user or team). Additionally, the Company may contain a plan or tier field if applicable (not explicit in the snippet, but sometimes CRMs track customer tier or plan). Audit fields (created_at, etc.) will track creation and modifications.

Enabled Workflows: Company records drive account-centric workflows. For sales, this means an Account Manager can see all deals associated with a given company and track the overall relationship. The CRM supports linking Contacts to Companies (via ContactCompanyRelationship), enabling workflows where sales reps navigate from a company to see all related contacts and their roles. Also, deals are typically associated with a company although in our schema, the Deal object did not show a direct company_id field, which is an interesting omission (Tickets had company_id, but Deals didnt in the snippet). Its possible that deals might link to companies via the Association table or that the design expects a deal to always have at least one associated contact and infer company through that contact. This could be a slight design divergence from classic CRMs where deals often have a primary company. However, given the presence of Company and these links, workflows such as pipeline reviews by account are intended: e.g., Show all open deals for Company X can be achieved by finding deals associated to contacts of that company or via association records. On the support side, having company on Ticket allows organization-based support: an agent can see all tickets from employees of Company X, and companies could have support-level attributes (like an SLA or support contract) applied. Indeed, in GroupProfile we saw a default_sla_policy_id that might apply by group, but often SLA could also vary by company (if big customers have enhanced SLAs). The schema doesnt directly store SLA info on company, but a companys tickets can inherit SLA through other means (like via group or product). Companies also facilitate marketing segmentation: you might filter contacts or deals by company industry or size. The Company fields like industry or custom categories support workflows such as targeting all companies in a certain vertical for a campaign. The notes on Company allow account managers or support to log company-level notes (Customer since 2020; key account). Company records also likely integrate with external data (e.g. enrichment from a business database) the schema might not explicitly show that, but fields like domain (website) make it possible to plug into enrichment services.

Lifecycle & Signals: A company record usually persists as long as the business is relevant (could be deleted if no longer a customer or merged if duplicate). Key signals include creation (when a new company is onboarded maybe via signing up or being added by sales), updates (change of name due to rebrand, ownership changes, etc.), and potentially an archival or deletion event. Theres no explicit status or active field on companies; typically if a company is no longer active, youd either delete it or mark via a custom field or use the existence of deals/tickets to gauge. The schema doesnt show an is_active on company (unlike on pipeline or forms), so likely not. However, a pattern could be using a List to mark inactive companies or using a tag via CompanyNote. The Owned_by fields provide a lifecycle of account ownership e.g., if owned_by_user_id changes, that signals an account re-assignment. If the platform had a concept of account health or renewal dates, those would be additional fields or separate objects (not present here, perhaps considered part of a Revenue or subscription system beyond CRM). For signals, integration events are likely emitted: e.g. company.created with all fields (the tenant integration guide snippet shows events for tenant, but presumably CRM would similarly emit events). Those would alert downstream systems of new accounts.

This relationship is crucial: it means a Company can have any number of Contacts, and a Contact can be linked to multiple companies (for instance, a consultant who works with multiple firms or a person who changed jobs historically one might create a new contact for new job, but the schema allows linking one contact to multiple companies if needed). Contact-Company Relationship

Concept & Purpose: The ContactCompany Relationship object is a linking entity that associates a Contact with a Company in a many-to-many fashion, including a descriptor of the relationship. It exists to model the role or connection a person has to an organization. In CRM terms, this is how the system knows John Doe is an Employee of Acme Corp or Jane Smith is the Billing Contact for XYZ Inc.. This intermediate object prevents having to assign a single company on the contact or vice versa, thereby supporting complex scenarios: a contact can be related to multiple companies (consultants, board members, etc.), and a company can have many contacts (employees, stakeholders).

Structure & Fields: The contact_company_relationship table typically has: an id (primary key), a tenant_id (for scoping), a contact_id (foreign key to Contact), a company_id (foreign key to Company), and a relationship_type field. The relationship_type is a short text (up to 50 chars as per schema) describing the nature of the relationship. The schema indicates this field is NOT NULL, meaning every link must classify the connection (examples might be employee, manager, point_of_contact, owner, etc.). The design likely expects a controlled vocabulary or at least consistent usage for relationship_type, but its stored as text (which provides flexibility to define new types without altering schema). There might also be audit fields (created_at, etc.), though the snippet only explicitly showed up to relationship_type. Its common to include timestamps to know when that link was created (e.g. when the contact joined the company). If a contact leaves a company, one might delete or mark the relationship accordingly (no explicit active flag, so deletion would be the clean way, though that loses history unless logged elsewhere). The relationship object doesnt hold more details than type (so no start/end date fields in this schema if needed that could be an extension).

Enabled Workflows: This relationship object enables organizational hierarchy workflows within CRM. For example:

  • For marketing, you might filter contacts by company attributes (via the link). For instance, Find all contacts whose company is in industry = Finance and who have relationship_type = decision-maker to target for a campaign.

  • The CRM likely exposes this through API and UI as the way to connect contacts and accounts. For example, an API to add a contact to a company would essentially create this relationship.

But the platform chose to have a dedicated table, likely because contact-company is a first-class link in CRM usage, deserving of its own endpoints and constraints. The domain model is clear that contact-company is an understood relation, not an ad-hoc one. There is no misuse of this table for anything else; its focused and appropriately named.

Competitive Parity: Handling contact to account relationships is standard in B2B CRM. Salesforce doesnt actually allow a contact to relate to multiple accounts without enabling a special feature (Account Contact Roles), which is analogous to this: they have a separate table for Contact Roles on Accounts to allow multi-account links, separate from the primary account field on contact. This CRMs approach is essentially that model by default it does not embed a single company field in Contact, instead using a join table for all. That is more flexible and on par with enterprise needs. HubSpot recently introduced multiple company association per contact with labeled associations; this matches that capability directly. The presence of the relationship_type is crucial for parity, as it lets you mark someones role relative to the company (e.g. you might mark one contact as the Decision Maker for sales vs another as Evaluator). Many CRMs have a concept of a primary contact or contact roles on deals; here we have it on accounts, which is good. To highlight: Zendesk (for support) historically allowed one organization per user, but newer updates allow multiple organizations per user with roles this design accommodates that better than older systems by nature. There isnt an explicit primary company field on contact, which means if needed, one of the relationships can serve as primary by convention (or they would use a specific relationship_type to denote primary). Thats an acceptable approach.

Platform Service Augmentation: This linking entity itself is straightforward and likely fully managed within the CRM service (via its API). Platform involvement might be:

  • Orchestration: might manage business logic when these links are created or removed. The Tenant Management or other services are not directly involved except ensuring the underlying contact and company exist.

  • Tenant Service: not directly, since these are CRM domain entities, but tenant_id ensures multi-tenancy. No cross-service mapping needed, as both contacts and companies are internal CRM objects.

  • Analytics: how these relationships evolve could be relevant to churn or org charts but nothing specific needed beyond events.

  • AI: Possibly, an AI might infer relationship types or suggest adding a contact to a company based on email domain matching. If implemented, the AI service could propose new links (but creation still happens through CRM).

  • Security: In a multi-tenant environment, these relationships don't leak data across tenants thanks to tenant scoping. That is in place as a platform concern (the database is likely partitioned by tenant or at least the queries always filter by tenant context).

  • If there were a Directory or HR integration (like syncing from an identity provider the list of employees), that could feed into contact-company relationships. But that would likely be a custom integration.

Overall, the Contact-Company Relationship object is a simple but essential join that bolsters the CRMs capability to represent real-world connections, and it is implemented in a credible, enterprise-ready way.

Company Relationship (Inter-Company)

Concept & Usage: The Company Relationship object (table company_relationship) defines links between two Company records, with a specified relationship type. This is used to model relationships between organizations, such as corporate hierarchies (parent/subsidiary), partnerships, or any custom link an enterprise might need (e.g. competitor, vendor). Its presence shows the CRMs ability to capture complex account structures beyond a flat list of companies, supporting enterprise sales strategies (like global parent account management) and support entitlements that might be inherited in a corporate family.

Structure & Fields: The schema of company_relationship includes an id (primary key), tenant_id, from_company_id, to_company_id, and a relationship_type (varchar 50). Essentially, its a directed relationship from one company to another, labeled by type. For example, a record could say: from_company = Acme Subsidiary Inc., to_company = Acme Corporation, relationship_type = "Subsidiary". Or from = Acme, to = Beta Corp, type = "Partner". The directed nature (from_company_id and to_company_id) means the relationship has an orientation which may or may not be inherently symmetric depending on type. (They might treat some types as symmetric like "partner" but the schema doesnt enforce symmetry, so if needed either they'd input reciprocal records or interpret type accordingly). Likely, for hierarchy, one direction is parent->child. The table likely has a composite unique constraint (tenant_id, from_company_id, to_company_id, relationship_type) to avoid duplicate identical relationships. Audit fields might exist similarly (not shown but presumably created_at etc.). Theres no extra metadata beyond type; so if one needed to know percentage ownership or something, thats not stored here its purely structural info.

Enabled Workflows: This inter-company link supports a few important workflows:

  • Account Hierarchy Management: Sales organizations often roll up multiple related customer entities into a single parent account for reporting or coordination. For example, if selling to a conglomerate with many subsidiaries, a sales director might want to see all deals across the conglomerate. With the relationships recorded, the CRM can aggregate pipeline or revenue data up the chain. Similarly, support might escalate issues from a subsidiary to the parents account team. The CRM UI can allow navigation: view a company record and see its Related Companies (children, parent, affiliates), giving account managers a full picture.

  • Inherited Attributes: The CRM could use parent relationships to inherit certain settings. For instance, if the parent company has a global contract, maybe all child companies get the same support SLA or pricing tier. While the schema doesnt explicitly enforce inheritance, an application rule could lookup parent relationships. In fact, in the GroupProfile object we saw a default_sla_policy_id and routing_config at the group level for support, but not at company. However, a typical use-case: if Company A is a subsidiary of B, maybe Bs priority status applies to A. The CRM or Orchestration could implement that by checking this table when creating a ticket or deal.

  • Partner/Channel Management: If using relationship_type for something like channel partner links (e.g., linking a reseller company to the end-customer company), the CRM can facilitate workflows where deals are attributed to partner relationships. For example, a partner manager might use the CRM to track which partner brought which client the partner company and client company could be linked as Partner->Customer relationship. Then reports could be generated per partner.

  • Duplicate Organization Handling: Sometimes, multiple entries for essentially the same org are created (subsidiaries, etc.). Instead of merging them, one could mark one as parent and others as branches. This table could then be used in data stewardship workflows to indicate which record is authoritative (with type like Alias or MergedInto), though thats speculative and not the primary intent (the primary intent is hierarchical, not aliasing).

  • Enterprise Reporting: The table allows queries like give me all companies that are children of X used in enterprise roll-up reporting. Also, if an account executive owns a parent, perhaps they implicitly oversee all subsidiaries (the CRM could enforce that by also linking owner groups or by query).

Lifecycle & Signals: Creating a company_relationship record signals an organizational change or recognition (like acquiring a new subsidiary or establishing a partnership). Deleting it signals perhaps a divestment or end of partnership. Theres no state or status on the relationship itself, other than existence. Changes are likely infrequent, and when they do happen, they should be communicated (the system could emit events like company_relationship.created/updated/deleted for integration or internal logic). Each relationship is tenant-scoped, so these changes are within one tenants context (if an MSP uses the platform, each of their client structures is separate, etc.). Theres likely consideration for preventing cycles (you wouldnt want A -> B and B -> A both unless you intentionally allow something like competitors which could be reciprocal in concept). The schema doesnt enforce acyclic graphs or anything that would be up to application logic. If used for hierarchy, we assume the users/admins manage it sanely.

Relationships: This is another join-like table, linking Company to Company. It has foreign keys for both from_company_id and to_company_id pointing to company.id (with tenant matching). So a company can have multiple outgoing relationships and multiple incoming. If representing a hierarchy, you might typically have one parent per child, but the schema doesnt forbid multiple (like Company A could be a subsidiary of two parents in data, though that likely wouldnt be used that way conceptually). Theres no direct link to contacts or deals here, except through the companies themselves. Also, no direct link to contact-company relationships; however, one could conceive that if Company A is parent of B, and John is linked to B, you might by inheritance consider John also belongs to parent A (but not recorded, just logical). The CRM might not automatically propagate that; but an Orchestration could if needed (like add contact of subsidiary to parents sphere).

No involvement of user accounts here aside from indirectly (the relationships may be managed by users with proper roles, but not stored here).

Overlap & Clarity: The CompanyRelationship object stands distinct from other objects. It is specifically for company-to-company links, which are not handled by any other table (Association could theoretically be used, but again, the presence of this dedicated table suggests that for inter-company links, this is the authoritative source, not the generic association). This separation is wise, as inter-company relationships often have different semantics and can be traversed recursively (like building a tree), which is easier when you know all relevant data is in one place. There is minimal risk of confusion with contact-company (different table and concept) or with association (which ideally is used for cross-entity links not covered by specialized ones).

Competitive Parity: Not all CRMs have strong support for company hierarchies out-of-the-box; Salesforce does (Parent Account field, which is a simpler model of one-level hierarchy, though they allow chaining it), Microsoft Dynamics does, HubSpot introduced a single parent link. This CRMs model is more flexible by allowing an arbitrary number of relationship types and presumably multiple link levels. This is very enterprise-friendly. It means users can represent complex org charts (like a parent with many children, each with further subdivisions, etc.) and even non-hierarchical relations if needed (like partner networks). That is something high-end CRMs support with extra configuration; here its built-in. The only nuance is that the CRM must ensure usability e.g., providing a UI to visualize these relationships but from a data perspective, its capable. If anything, a competitor like HubSpot historically lacked multi-level account hierarchies (though they added a single parent field later). This design surpasses that by supporting multiple relationship categories. No major gaps unless one expected more fields on the relationship (like effective dates or ownership percentages). But those can be custom if needed; core functionality is there.

Platform Service Augmentation: The actual storage and retrieval of these relationships is within CRM. Platform services might come into play as:

  • Orchestration: Use the relationship data for processes. For example, if a high severity ticket comes in from Subsidiary X, Orchestration might escalate it not only to Xs owner but also to the parent companys owner or group, by traversing this relationship. The Orchestration engine can query the CRM via API for any parent relationships (theres no precomputed roll-up in schema, so it would have to navigate).

  • Reporting/BI: A separate analytics service would likely leverage these relationships for rolled-up reporting. The CRM might not do the summation itself, but an analytics pipeline could aggregate Total pipeline by parent account by walking these links.

  • Data integration: If the company structure is maintained in an external system (like a master data management tool), those relationships might flow into CRM. The CRM would then update the company_relationship records accordingly (perhaps through an Integration service or a scheduled job).

  • Tenant/Access Control: Possibly, if certain teams only handle certain subsidiaries, an RBAC system might reference these relationships to allow a user with rights on parent to see children, etc. The schema doesnt enforce or mention that, but its a plausible usage through application logic.

  • The design assumes no need for cross-tenant linking (which is correct relationships are only relevant within one tenants data).

In summary, the CompanyRelationship domain object is a straightforward yet powerful construct to mirror real-world corporate structures. Its inclusion demonstrates a capacity to handle enterprise scenarios such as hierarchical accounts and complex account ecosystems, which is consistent with capabilities in platforms like Salesforce. The CRM service would handle the CRUD of these relationships internally, relying on Orchestration or external analytics to exploit them for automation or reporting where needed.

Deal

Concept & Business Role: A Deal represents a sales opportunity or transaction in progress, typically tracking potential revenue. Its the central object in the sales pipeline, moving through stages defined by Pipeline/Pipeline Stage. In CRM workflows, deals correspond to things like potential contracts, sales leads that have been qualified and are now being worked, or opportunities. They serve as the bridge between contacts (people) and revenue capturing what is being sold, to whom, and the likelihood and timeline of closing the sale.

Structure & Fields: The Deal schema contains fields for the core attributes of a sales opportunity:

  • Name: A descriptive name for the deal (often something like Company X - Product Y Purchase). This is required.

  • Amount: The potential value of the deal (monetary). Its optional or null if unknown.

  • Expected Close Date: A date the sales rep estimates the deal will close. (Format date, can be null).

  • Pipeline & Stage: Foreign keys pipeline_id and stage_id link the deal into a specific pipeline and current stage. These are required, meaning every deal must be in a stage of a pipeline from creation.

  • Probability: Likely a computed or manually set percentage chance of closing, which might override or complement the stages default probability. If not provided, it might default from stage; if provided, it could represent a more fine-grained control (some CRMs let reps adjust probability).

  • Deal Type: A categorical field (string) to classify the deal (e.g. New Business vs Upsell). Not required, but helps segmentation.

  • Forecast Probability: Possibly a separate figure used for forecasting (could be system-calculated, different from reps probability).

  • Close Date: The actual closing date (perhaps blank until deal is won/lost).

  • Assigned User/Group: There are assigned_user_id and assigned_group_id fields. These indicate who is currently responsible for the deal (owner in sales context can be different from who originally owned the contact or account for instance, a sales engineer group might be temporarily assigned). The presence of both suggests deals can be assigned to an individual or team (similar to tickets in support).

  • Owned By User/Group: Additionally, deals have owned_by_user_id and owned_by_group_id. This is interesting because assigned vs. owned might indicate a distinction between overall account owner and current deal handler. Possibly, owned_by is the account owner (like who ultimately gets quota credit or is the AE) and assigned_to might be used for current workflow (like BDR or support roles). The schema includes both, which is robust for complex orgs (e.g., a team might own a deal but assign tasks to specific users).

  • Audit Fields: id, tenant_id, created_at, updated_at, created_by, updated_by are present.

All required fields on creation: name, pipeline_id, stage_id (and by extension pipeline) are mandatory, ensuring deals always live in a pipeline stage. The numeric fields like amount and probability can be null (unknown) and updated later.

Notably missing in the deal schema is an explicit link to a Company or Contact. Thats a key relational aspect: normally, deals are associated with a company (account) and often a primary contact. This schema likely expects those links to be handled via the generic Association table for contacts or companies. The absence of company_id in Deal suggests if one wanted to find which company the deal is for, one must either:

  • Find an Association of type "deal" to "company".

  • Or find the contacts linked to that deal via Association, then their company.

This is a more flexible but less straightforward approach compared to having a direct company field. It may reflect a design where deals could potentially involve multiple companies (like partner deals). Or simply they rely on contacts as proxies. In any event, its a difference from typical CRMs (Salesforce has an Account field on Opportunity; HubSpot has Company and Contact associations directly on deal). This CRM chooses not to enforce a single account link, instead leaving it open via associations. The ListObjectType included "deal", meaning deals can be part of Lists (for segmentation or bulk operations).

Enabled Workflows: Deals power sales pipeline management workflows:

  • Pipeline Progression: As deals move through stages (controlled by stage_id), the CRM tracks their advancement. Reps update deals as they move closer to closing, and those updates (stage changes) trigger events and potentially StageHistory records (though StageHistory is generic, one would use entity_type "deal" in StageHistory to log each stage change, which the change request file does include StageHistory with entity_type field).

  • Quota and Forecasting: The amount, probability, expected_close_date, and stage probabilities allow calculation of expected revenue in time periods. Sales managers use this for forecasting. The forecast_probability field suggests a separate computed chance (perhaps adjusted by management or AI). Combined, these fields support advanced forecasting workflows (like commit vs best case forecasting).

  • Task and Activity Management: Deals often have tasks (calls, meetings) associated. The CRMs Activity object can be tied to a deal likely via an association or a field (not shown). Reps will log communications via Activity on a deal, which the CRM can present in a timeline. The structure allows that because deals and activities both can be tied to a contact or to each other via associations. (For example, an Association might link an Activity to a Deal if needed).

  • Assignment & Handoff: The presence of separate owned_by vs assigned_user suggests workflows like: Owned_by_group = "Sales Team A", assigned_user = a specific rep or perhaps a solutions engineer at some stage. A deal might initially be assigned to a junior rep (assigned_user) but owned by a senior (owned_by_user for oversight). If the deal enters a certain stage, assignment might change (maybe assigned_group becomes "Legal Team" for contract review at end). The schemas flexibility here enables those handoff workflows.

  • Collaborative Selling: Because a deal can have an assigned group, it supports scenarios like team selling or overlay teams. Also, multiple contacts can be associated to the deal (stakeholders), and those contacts can belong to various companies if needed (like a partner rep from another firm involved one could link both the customer and partner contacts to the deal).

  • Deal Tracking and Reporting: The CRM can generate pipeline reports (how many deals in each stage, total amounts by stage, etc.) because every deal is in a stage and has an amount. Weighted pipeline is computed via probability * amount, which is directly supported. Close date allows timeline reports (e.g., expected sales this quarter). The multiple probability fields allow reps to override stage probability or use forecast categories separate from stage (similar to how Salesforce has "Forecast Category" vs "Probability", this design has "forecast_probability" as perhaps system override).

  • Deal Closure & Outcomes: Although not explicitly in fields, the final outcome of a deal (won, lost, etc.) is likely indicated by moving to a pipeline stage that is designated a closing stage (like a stage with stage_state CLOSED_WON or CLOSED_LOST as indicated by PipelineStages stage_state field). Alternatively, they might have expected to use an association or a field for outcome but none is visible except the stage. So presumably marking a deal as won means moving it to a stage with probability 100% and perhaps closing it. The actual revenue closed might equal amount (unless partial, but partials not indicated in schema).

Lifecycle & Signals: Deals progress through a lifecycle from creation (open) to closed (either won or lost). Lifecycle signals include:

  • Stage transitions: Each move is a critical event, presumably logged in StageHistory (with entity_type deal) including timestamps and who changed it. This gives a history of the sales cycle timeline.

  • Ownership or assignment changes: If a deal is reassigned, thats a significant internal event. The assigned_by_user_id and reason in TicketAssignmentOut suggests a similar concept might exist for deals if they had an assignment history, but they did not list a DealAssignment table. Likely, assignment changes for deals might be simply updates on the deal record (with updated_by capturing who did it). Or they might reuse TicketAssignment for deals if they designed it generically (but it's named TicketAssignment, likely not reused).

  • Closure: When a deal is marked closed-won or lost, typically a field or status changes. Here, it might be indicated by stage (e.g., stage name = "Closed Won" or "Closed Lost"). The closed_at date field is present, which presumably is set when the deal is closed. This field is likely filled programmatically when the stage changes to a closing stage (the CRM might set closed_at = now). That provides a clear signal of the deals end date. For lost deals, they might still set closed_at and maybe keep probability 0 or stage with stage_state "CLOSED_LOST".

  • There is no explicit lost reason or notes field in schema, but reps could record that in a Note entity or a custom field if needed.

  • The audit fields track record modifications, and presumably events are emitted: e.g., crm.deal.created, crm.deal.updated (with changes), crm.deal.deleted for rare deletion (maybe if a deal was entered erroneously).

  • If deals are often not deleted but archived, theres no is_archived on deal, but the is_active field is not present either. Possibly, closed deals remain in system indefinitely or until manually deleted. Archival could be managed by moving to a closed stage but if they wanted to hide them, they'd rely on filters rather than a flag, since none exists by default.

Relationships:

  • Pipeline & Stage: As mentioned, each Deal must relate to a Pipeline and a PipelineStage (the foreign keys for these are core to deals). Without a valid pipeline stage, a deal cant exist. The schema ensures referential integrity here, so a stage cannot be deleted if deals reference it (or at least you'd have to reassign deals).

  • Contacts: Deals typically involve one or more contacts. This CRM doesnt have a direct contact field on deal, but the Association table would be used. For instance, to link Contact ID 123 as a stakeholder on Deal ID 456, an association could be created: from_object_type = contact, from_object_id = 123, to_object_type = deal, to_object_id = 456, association_type = maybe Decision Maker or Influencer. The OpenAPI indeed references PaginationEnvelope_DealRead_ and has an association that list membership is allowed, but doesnt show a direct association management, implying associations are separate. Nevertheless, since Association exists for general links, its the logical method for tying deals to contacts. The data model thus supports multiple contacts per deal (just add multiple association records, possibly with different roles).

  • Companies: Similarly, linking a deal to a company would either be done by association or by inferring from associated contacts company. If they intended a more explicit link, they might have considered adding a company_id on deal but chose not to. So likely, for clarity, one might create an Association from deal to company (or simply rely on contacts company in queries). Given company Relationship exists, to get all deals for a parent company, you might have to gather deals for that company and its subsidiaries by collating all contacts or associations somewhat complex, but doable with the data available.

  • Owner/Assigned Entities: The owned_by_user_id/group_id and assigned_user_id/group_id link to internal user accounts and groups. Those relationships are enforced by foreign keys via the shadow tables (the change script presumably adds those constraints as it did for contact and others). This ensures that any user or group referenced actually exists in the tenant. It also ties deals into user workflows: e.g. a users task list might include all open deals assigned to them, which you get by filtering deals on assigned_user_id.

  • Activities & Notes: Sales reps will often have notes or tasks on deals. The CRM likely uses the Activity object for tasks/calls related to a deal. Possibly an Activity could have a field referencing a deal or more likely uses associations (like an association linking activity to deal, plus maybe to contact). For notes specifically, they did not define a DealNote separate table (Contact and Company have distinct Note tables; deals might rely on Activity of type note or the generic association where a contact note isnt appropriate). Alternatively, they might allow linking a ContactNote to a deal via association, but thats a bit stretchy. If needed, they could have used the general notes concept or repurposed the Activity as internal notes.

  • List Membership: Deals can be part of Lists. For example, a sales manager might create a dynamic list of All deals expected to close this month over $100k. The List and ListMembership schema allows that since member_type can be "deal". That implies an ability to filter deals by criteria (the filter_definition on List would specify conditions on deal fields). So deals relate to lists through ListMembership entries, providing segmentation for reporting or bulk updates.

  • Ticket or Support Links: Not typical, but sometimes a support case might be linked to a deal (e.g. a big deal might have an open technical issue that could affect it). No explicit linking is given for deals to tickets, but if needed, the Association object could link a ticket to a deal with a certain type (like Related Issue). This doesnt seem a primary design, but the flexible association means cross-domain references are possible if they arise (the system doesnt restrict what object types association can connect).

  • Automation & Workflows: The AutomationAction rules might target deals too (with entity_type "deal" in the rule). For instance, trigger events might include deal.updated and conditions like stage changed to Closed Won to trigger a celebration workflow or notifying Finance. The schema for AutomationAction lists pipeline_id and stage_id as optional filters in a rule, which strongly suggests automation can specifically target deals in certain pipelines or stages (e.g. when a deal enters stage X, do Y). This ties deals into orchestrated processes (like automatically creating an onboarding project when a deal is won).

  • Record Watchers: If internal users follow a deal to get updates, the RecordWatcher object might be used. The RecordWatcher can subscribe a principal (user) to changes in a record given its record_type (like deal) and record_id. This means the platform supports watchers across different objects generically, so watchers on deals is likely supported via that. Its not a direct relationship in the data model of the deal itself, but a related service feature.

Overlap & Gaps: The Deal object stands on its own in responsibilities no other object duplicates its function of tracking opportunities. There is some domain coupling with PipelineStage which is by design. The interesting gap is the lack of direct Company/Contact foreign key which is normally expected on an opportunity. They rely on Associations for that. This could be seen as a complexity (requiring joins on a separate table to get account information for a deal). However, it may be a deliberate choice to allow deals to link to multiple entities (like multi-company deals or consortia). It does raise a domain risk: if associations arent consistently created, you might have deals floating with no easily accessible account context. The CRM integrators must treat adding associations as part of deal creation (the OpenAPI probably enforces or encourages linking a deal to something). For example, maybe the Deal create API might allow passing a contact or company to associate at creation even though the DB doesnt have the field (the service would under the hood create the association). If not, it relies on subsequent calls to link. We should flag that as a potential integration pain point: the schema is flexible, but integrators must know to use it properly (possible domain leakage if they misuse association for things that have first-class fields in other CRMs).

Another minor note: There is no explicit currency field on deal. Possibly they assume a single currency or have multi-currency offloaded to configuration (some systems have a currency code per amount). If multi-currency is needed, the schema doesnt show it, which might be a gap for global enterprises. But this is something that could be handled at application layer (all deals in tenants default currency, etc.).

Competitive Parity: The Deal object hits the key points: value, close dates, stage, probabilities, assignments. This is very similar to Salesforces Opportunity or HubSpots Deal. The dual assignment (user and group) is a plus for team workflows (Salesforce requires additional sharing rules or team objects for similar effect; here its built-in to assign a group). The absence of a direct account field is a divergence, as noted, but functionally it can achieve the same outcome through associations its just a matter of convenience vs flexibility. In practice, enterprise customers often demand seeing which account a deal is for; this CRM can do it (just has to join association or always include that in queries). Provided the CRM service and UI handle that seamlessly, its fine. The presence of a close_date and expected_close_date covers forecasting timelines thoroughly, on par with advanced CRMs where expected close and actual close are tracked. They did not show a field for stage status or outcome explicitly (like won/lost), but thats encoded by stage. Thats standard (e.g., in Salesforce, an Opportunitys stage being a Closed Won type also sets a separate flag IsClosed = true; here they can infer from stage_state maybe, but they haven't shown a computed field for "is_closed"). Possibly they rely on stages stage_state or simply the presence of closed_at being not null to indicate closed. Either way, parity is mostly there.

Platform Service Augmentation: The Deal entity engages several platform services:

  • Orchestration Service: Key for automating sales processes. For example, when a deal moves stage, Orchestration could create a sequence of tasks (using the integration with the Task Mirror if they had tasks tied to deals like they do with TicketTaskMirror for support). We dont see a DealTaskMirror, but if Orchestration is used for tasks, it might still mirror tasks per ticket but not deals, unclear. However, given TicketTaskMirror is specific, deals might not use that pattern. Still, Orchestration could assign tasks to users or update other systems when deals progress or close. The presence of orchestration fields on Ticket (workflow_key, etc.) indicates heavy integration on support side; none on deal suggests maybe the sales flows are considered simpler or orchestrated in a lighter way (maybe via AutomationAction rules in-app).

  • AI Workforce: Could be used for deal scoring or next best action suggestions. For example, an AI model might update a custom field or the forecast_probability on a deal. The CRM has the forecast_probability field which might be where an AIs prediction is stored (distinct from rep-entered probability). That synergy is plausible: e.g. an AI service calculates a 30% likelihood to close, writes that in forecast_probability, while the rep might still put 50% manually. This separation is advanced and shows foresight for AI-assisted forecasting.

  • Tenant Service: Like others, for owner/assignment referencing valid users and groups. The change logs likely added FKs for owned_by and assigned_to fields referencing tenant_user_shadow and tenant_group_shadow as we saw for contacts. That means if a user is removed, a cascade might break or be prevented until deals reassigned; presumably the platforms user management would handle that by event (maybe Orchestration auto-unassigns or marks something).

  • Analytics/BI: Complex sales metrics (pipeline velocity, conversion rates, average deal size) would be computed outside just the transactional DB. But the schema provides the raw data needed. It might rely on external analysis to, for example, measure how long deals stay in each stage (though StageHistory logging helps with that data inside CRM).

  • Campaign Manager/Cross-sell: If marketing wants to target deals (less common; usually target contacts or companies), lists allow deals grouping maybe for internal processes or data cleanup rather than campaigning.

  • Document/Templating: If a deal requires generating a proposal or contract doc, the Templating Service would use deal fields (like amount, close date, etc.) and contact/company info to produce a document. The CRM itself doesnt generate docs; it would call out to templating with context. There is no direct integration object for that, but possibly the user would trigger such from UI.

Activity

Concept & Scope: An Activity represents a scheduled or completed action related to sales or support, such as a task, call, meeting, email, etc., that typically a user performs. Its a general-purpose object for tracking interactions and to-dos. In the CRM, Activities serve to log communications and plan follow-ups, enabling a timeline of engagement for contacts, companies, or deals. They often feed into both sales productivity (ensuring tasks are done) and relationship tracking (seeing all touchpoints with a customer).

Structure & Fields: From the OpenAPI, the Activity object includes:

  • Type: a classification of the activity (string up to 20). Likely values: Call, Meeting, Task, Email, etc. This is required on create, meaning every activity must have a defined type.

  • Title: a short summary or subject of the activity. Optional (can be null).

  • Description: a longer text describing the activity details or notes. Optional.

  • Due Date: for tasks, a date by which it should happen or did happen. Optional.

There is no field like contact_id or deal_id shown in Activity. This implies that linking an activity to a particular contact or deal is likely done via the Association object (the generic link table). E.g., to log a call with a contact regarding a deal, one might create an Activity (type=Call, description) and then create Association entries: one linking the activity to the contact, another linking it to the deal. The absence of direct linking fields keeps Activity generic, able to be associated with any object type as needed. This design is flexible (one activity can be linked to multiple objects, e.g. a meeting with two contacts about two deals) but requires those association records to be managed.
